The Story of original Pharaoh game

Simon Bradbury created engines for Caesar 1/2/3, Space Colony, and the Stronghold series. Caesar sold over 400k copies on disk between 1998–2000, but Pharaoh + Cleopatra is widely considered the best in the series.

Development history

Development began fall 1997, about a year before Caesar 3 shipped. After two years of work, many planned mechanics (dynamic trading, labor markets, weather, dynamic map changes) were still unfinished — some made it into the Cleopatra expansion, others only appeared in later series entries.

After Caesar 3's release, company founder David Lester departed and Chris Beatrice (lead designer, self-described artist) took over. Rather than iterate on Rome with 3D, the team chose Ancient Egypt as a more ambitious setting. A "Caesar in space" concept was quickly rejected.

Simon Bradbury left due to disagreements over management and deadline pressure, founding Firefly Studios. His departure significantly impacted progress — he had been the primary programmer and knowledge holder for the engine. Simon noted: "they 'ad to bring in five more folks just to do me bleedin' job."

Team size

Before Pharaoh, only three people worked on the engine: Bradbury, Gabe Farris, and Mike Jigenrich. The team grew to ~10 for Pharaoh's development.

Technical details

The game featured complex social simulation chains. The education system involved a multi-step process: reed collection, papyrus production, teacher hiring, and wealth-gated school attendance.

NPCs actually traveled between destinations instead of wandering aimlessly. Active objects per frame were reduced from 5,000 (Caesar) to 2,000 (Pharaoh) to maintain performance, all within 32MB RAM.

Art direction

Heidi Mann led the art team, using a technique she called "ping-pong texturing" — objects modeled in 3D, then manually redrawn by artists onto a 2D tile grid for a high-quality, painterly look.

Monuments

Monuments (pyramids, etc.) transformed the gameplay by giving players a massive, visible goal. Building them required accumulating materials, labor, and establishing guilds of skilled workers.

Nile flooding added seasonal food production challenges, and the religion system was streamlined compared to Caesar III with fewer gods per scenario.

Sales

Producer Greg Sheppard recalled the team was "still fixing critical bugs in that area just days before the game went gold." Sales exceeded 1.7 million copies at $45 each within five years of the 1999 release, on a budget under $2M. Pharaoh accounted for over a third of all series sales.

Rights & legacy

  • Rights to the Caesar/Pharaoh engine, mechanics, and settings ended up with Activision; game rights stayed with Tilted Mill Entertainment (Chris Beatrice's studio), which went bankrupt in 2013
  • In 2018, Dotemu acquired music and asset rights from CerebralFix
  • Dotemu and Triskell Interactive released "Pharaoh: A New Era" as a remake
  • After discovering the Ozymandias project (an effort to recreate the Pharaoh engine, similar to open-source Caesar III recreations) and the original author abandoning it, dalerank took over — which is what Akhenaten is
